What Is an Annotated Bibliography?

An annotated bibliography is a list of citations to sources (books, articles, websites, etc.) where each citation is followed by a brief descriptive and evaluative paragraph—the annotation.

📚 A Regular Bibliography

A list of sources formatted in a citation style (APA, MLA, Chicago). It tells your reader what you consulted.

Smith, J. (2023). Social media and mental health. Journal of Psychology, 45(2), 112-130.

✍️ An Annotated Bibliography

The same citation plus a paragraph that summarizes, evaluates, and reflects on the source.

Smith, J. (2023). Social media and mental health...

This peer-reviewed study surveyed 2,000 college students and found a significant correlation between daily social media use and reported anxiety levels. The large sample size strengthens the findings, though the self-reported data is a limitation...

Why Write an Annotated Bibliography?

It may feel like busywork, but annotated bibliographies are one of the most useful exercises in the research process.

Benefits for You as a Researcher

1
Forces critical reading
You can't summarize and evaluate a source without actually reading and understanding it.
2
Organizes your research
Creates a reference document you can return to when writing your paper—no more re-reading sources from scratch.
3
Identifies gaps
Seeing all your sources together reveals what perspectives or evidence you're still missing.
4
Builds your argument early
Writing about how each source connects to your topic helps you start forming your thesis before the paper is due.
5
Demonstrates your research skills
Shows your professor that you can find, read, and think critically about scholarly sources.
💡 Pro Tip: Even when it's not assigned, creating an annotated bibliography for yourself is one of the best ways to stay organized on a big research project!

Types of Annotations

Not all annotations are the same. Your professor may ask for one specific type, so always check your assignment guidelines.

TypeWhat It DoesExample Phrase
SummaryDescribes the source's main argument, methods, and findings. Objective—no personal opinion."This study examines the relationship between sleep deprivation and academic performance among first-year college students using survey data from 500 participants..."
EvaluativeAssesses the source's quality, reliability, and limitations. Includes your critical judgment."While the sample size is strong, the study relies on self-reported data, which may introduce bias. The authors also do not account for..."
ReflectiveExplains how the source fits into your research. Connects it to your argument or thesis."This article will support my argument that universities should invest in sleep education programs by providing empirical evidence of the academic consequences..."
⚠️ Most professors want all three combined! A strong annotation typically summarizes the source, evaluates its strengths/weaknesses, and reflects on how you'll use it. When in doubt, include all three elements.

Anatomy of an Annotated Bibliography Entry

Every entry has two parts: the citation and the annotation. Here's what goes into each.

THE CITATION (formatted in your required style—APA, MLA, or Chicago)

Twenge, J. M., & Campbell, W. K. (2018). Associations between screen time and lower psychological well-being among children and adolescents. Preventive Medicine Reports, 12, 271–283. https://doi.org/10.1016/j.pmedr.2018.10.003

THE ANNOTATION (typically 100–200 words)
① SUMMARY: This large-scale study analyzed data from over 40,000 U.S. children and adolescents (ages 2–17) to examine the relationship between screen time and psychological well-being. The authors found that higher screen time was significantly associated with lower well-being, including less curiosity, lower self-control, more distractibility, difficulty making friends, less emotional stability, and increased difficulty finishing tasks.

② EVALUATION: The study's major strength is its massive sample size drawn from national survey data, which increases generalizability. However, the cross-sectional design means causation cannot be established—it is possible that children with lower well-being simply spend more time on screens. The reliance on caregiver-reported data for younger children may also introduce bias.

③ REFLECTION: This source directly supports my research on the effects of technology on adolescent development. I will use it to establish the scope of the problem in my literature review and to argue for age-appropriate screen time guidelines in educational settings.

Writing Strong Annotations

A good annotation is concise, specific, and analytical. Here's a framework to follow.

The 3-Part Formula

1
Summarize (2–3 sentences)
What is the source about? What is the main argument or finding? What methods were used?
Ask yourself: If someone hadn't read this, what would they need to know?
2
Evaluate (1–2 sentences)
Is the source reliable? What are its strengths and limitations? Is the evidence convincing?
Ask yourself: Should I trust this source? Why or why not?
3
Reflect (1–2 sentences)
How does this source fit your research? Does it support, challenge, or complicate your argument?
Ask yourself: Why am I including this in my bibliography?

Do's and Don'ts

✅ Do

  • Write in complete sentences
  • Be specific about findings and methods
  • Use your own words (no quotes)
  • Connect the source to your topic
  • Note the source type (study, review, report)
  • Stay within your word count (usually 100–200 words)

❌ Don't

  • Copy the abstract
  • Just say "this is a good source"
  • Summarize without evaluating
  • Write in first person unless told to
  • Include sources you haven't actually read
  • Forget to check your citation format

Common Mistakes to Avoid

These are the pitfalls that cost students the most points. Watch out for them!

🚫 Mistake 1: Copying the Abstract

The abstract is the author's summary. Your annotation should be your analysis in your words. Professors can spot a copied abstract immediately.

Fix: Read the full source (or at least the intro, results, and conclusion), then write your summary from memory.

🚫 Mistake 2: Summary Without Evaluation

Describing what a source says without assessing its quality or relevance is only doing half the job.

Fix: After summarizing, always ask: Is this source reliable? How does it help my specific argument?

🚫 Mistake 3: Vague or Generic Language

"This article talks about social media" tells your reader nothing. Be specific about what the source actually argues or finds.

Fix: Replace vague language with concrete details: sample sizes, key findings, specific arguments.

🚫 Mistake 4: Wrong Citation Format

Mixing up APA and MLA, missing hanging indents, or forgetting DOIs are easy points to lose.

Fix: Use Zotero or Purdue OWL to double-check every citation before submitting.

🚫 Mistake 5: Using Sources You Haven't Read

If you can only write a one-sentence summary, you probably haven't read enough of the source to annotate it.

Fix: At minimum, read the abstract, introduction, and conclusion of every source you include.

Tips for Efficient Source Finding

💡 Smart Strategies

  • Use Boolean operators (AND, OR, NOT)
  • Check the references of a good article to find more sources
  • Filter to peer-reviewed and recent (last 5–10 years)
  • Use subject headings from relevant articles

📅 Time Management

  • Don't try to find all sources in one sitting
  • Save citations to Zotero as you go
  • Read abstracts first to decide if a source is worth your time
  • Request ILL early for hard-to-find sources

Activity 1: Evaluate an Annotation

Which Annotation Is Better?

Read both annotations below for the same source, then answer the questions.

Citation: Orben, A., & Przybylski, A. K. (2019). The association between adolescent well-being and digital technology use. Nature Human Behaviour, 3(2), 173–182.

Annotation A

This article is about technology and well-being. It was published in Nature which is a good journal. The authors are researchers. This source is useful for my paper because it relates to my topic about social media and mental health. I will use this in my research.

Annotation B

Using three large-scale datasets totaling over 350,000 adolescents, Orben and Przybylski found that digital technology use accounts for only 0.4% of the variation in adolescent well-being—less than the negative effect of wearing glasses or eating potatoes. The study's use of Specification Curve Analysis, which tests all possible analytical combinations rather than cherry-picking results, represents a significant methodological advance. However, the cross-sectional data cannot establish causation. This source will complicate my argument by showing that screen time effects may be smaller than widely reported, requiring me to nuance my claims.

1. What is Annotation A missing?

2. What makes Annotation B strong? What do you notice?

APA 7th Edition: Quick Reference

Since APA is the most commonly required style at Tulane, here's a quick formatting guide.

Common Source Formats

# Journal Article (with DOI) Author, A. A., & Author, B. B. (Year). Title of article. Title of Periodical, Volume(Issue), Page–Page. https://doi.org/xxxxx # Book Author, A. A. (Year). Title of work: Capital letter also for subtitle. Publisher. # Chapter in Edited Book Author, A. A. (Year). Title of chapter. In E. E. Editor (Ed.), Title of book (pp. xx–xx). Publisher. # Website Author or Organization. (Year, Month Day). Title of page. Site Name. https://www.url.com

Formatting Rules

Page Layout

  • Double-spaced throughout
  • 1-inch margins all sides
  • Hanging indent (0.5") for each entry
  • Alphabetical by first author's last name
  • Title "References" centered and bold at top

Annotation Format

  • Begins on a new line after the citation
  • Indented 0.5" from left margin
  • Double-spaced, same as citation
  • 100–200 words (check your assignment)
  • Written in paragraph form
